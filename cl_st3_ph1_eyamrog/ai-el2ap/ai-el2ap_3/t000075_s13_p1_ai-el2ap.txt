The shift in mainstream perspectives on capital controls has been
gradual rather than abrupt. Approximately two decades ago, mainstream economic
thought predominantly supported full financial openness. The present study identifies
a progression in the focus of mainstream research, noting that
the currency and financial crises experienced by several emerging economies
in the late 1990s represented a significant turning point in
attitudes toward capital account liberalization. These crises, along with subsequent
financial disturbances in the early 2000s, prompted an increase in
empirical research that challenges the traditional assumptions regarding the benefits
